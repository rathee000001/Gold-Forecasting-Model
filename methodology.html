Methodology

A transparent, Excel-based workflow to forecast gold price behavior using monthly return modeling, validation through backtesting, and decision-relevant interpretation for Supply Chain Management.

Workflow: Data → Transformations → Regression Estimation → Backtesting → Forecast Use

Output: Excel model + Tableau dashboards + AI log documentation

Use this page to understand:

  • What data is used and why
  • How variables are transformed into returns and standardized inputs
  • How the model generates predicted returns and a price path
  • How validation is done via backtesting
  • Why the approach matters for supply chain decisions

1. Data Collection & Sources

The model uses a structured set of monthly time-series inputs that represent gold price behavior and key economic, currency, trade, geopolitical, and reserve-related drivers.

Target: Gold price in USD and derived monthly gold returns.

Macro: Interest-rate and inflation proxies (used to reflect real-rate conditions).

Currency: USD strength measures (because gold is USD-denominated).

Trade / Commodity Proxy: import/export or trade-linked indices related to gold movement.

Risk: Geopolitical risk indicators (shocks can drive flight-to-safety demand).

Reserves: Central bank reserve behavior (global and BRICS share changes).

2. Transformations & Preparation

To make time-series relationships more stable and comparable, the model converts raw series into returns and standardized inputs, then aligns all variables to the same monthly timeline.

Returns focus: The model analyzes returns rather than raw prices to reduce non-stationarity.

Log returns / percent change: Used to express month-to-month change consistently.

Standardization (Z-scores): Inputs are normalized so different scales don’t dominate the regression.

Alignment: All variables are synchronized to the same month index to prevent timing mismatches.

Quality controls: Checks for missing values and inconsistent dates to keep regression inputs valid.

3. Estimation Framework

The model uses a regression-based approach to estimate how changes in standardized factors relate to gold returns. Estimated coefficients produce a predicted return series, which can be converted into a modeled price path.

Regression logic: Gold return is modeled as a linear function of standardized factor returns.

Coefficient meaning: Each factor receives a weight based on historical relationships.

Prediction: A predicted return is generated each month using the estimated weights.

Constraints & assumptions: Linear structure, stable relationships, and monthly frequency.

4. Backtesting & Validation

Validation is performed by comparing model-implied returns (and the derived price path) against actual historical gold behavior. The goal is to evaluate reliability, directionality, and practical usefulness.

Purpose: Test whether the model tracks major movements and turning points.

Accuracy vs usefulness: Direction and regime detection can matter more than perfect price matching.

Error analysis: Differences often occur during shocks, structural breaks, and sudden risk events.

Decision lens: A good model helps anticipate risk exposure and hedging timing in supply chains.

5. Supply Chain Relevance

Gold price forecasting supports supply chain decision-making when organizations face commodity exposure, global risk, currency impacts, and procurement timing challenges.

Procurement planning: Better timing of purchases for gold-linked inputs and materials.

Hedging strategy: Informing hedge triggers and the timing of financial protection.

Risk management: Monitoring macro + geopolitical conditions that affect commodity costs.

Policy & reserves lens: Understanding how reserve accumulation and risk events influence demand.

6. Limitations & Enhancements

The model is intentionally transparent and reproducible, but it also carries constraints typical of monthly, linear factor-based forecasting approaches.

Linearity: Relationships may change under extreme market stress or regime shifts.

Monthly frequency: Reduces noise but may miss fast-moving events within a month.

Structural breaks: Sudden shifts can cause temporary divergence from actual prices.

Enhancements: Additional scenario design, robustness checks, and alternative specifications can improve stability.
