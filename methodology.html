Model Framework

This project builds a transparent, Excel-based monthly gold price level forecasting model using macro-financial drivers. We document data sourcing, cleaning, month-end alignment, regression estimation, and backtesting (in-sample and out-of-sample) to produce an interpretable and academically defensible workflow.

Workflow: Data → Cleaning & Month-End Alignment → Regression (Price Level) → Backtesting → Conditional Forecast

Output: Excel model + Tableau dashboards + AI log documentation

1. History of Gold & Monetary Role

Gold is often treated as a monetary asset because it has historically served as a store of value and a reserve asset held by central banks. In modern markets, gold can respond to inflation expectations, real/nominal interest rates, currency strength (USD), and risk regimes (volatility and geopolitical shocks).

Monetary behavior: Gold can act like “financial insurance” in periods of stress and policy uncertainty.

USD-denominated pricing: A stronger USD often pressures gold; a weaker USD can support it.

Reserves channel: Central bank purchases/sales affect long-run demand and regime dynamics.

2. Why Gold (vs other metals)

  • Not purely industrial: Unlike copper/aluminum, gold demand is heavily financial and reserve-driven.
  • Macro sensitivity: Gold is more directly linked to rates, inflation, USD strength, and risk sentiment.
  • Policy relevance: Gold is actively held by central banks as a reserve asset, which supports a macro-factor approach.

3. Data Sources & Rationale (with Links)

Each variable is selected to represent a distinct macro-financial channel that influences gold prices: opportunity cost, inflation protection, currency valuation, market risk, geopolitical uncertainty, and structural demand from central banks.

Gold Price (USD/oz): World Gold Council / LBMA — gold.org/goldhub/data/gold-prices
Why used: This is the dependent variable of the model. LBMA prices are the global benchmark used in academic research and financial markets. Month-end gold prices reflect prevailing macro and risk conditions priced by the market at that point in time.

US10Y Nominal Yield (GS10, monthly): FRED — fred.stlouisfed.org/series/GS10
Why used: The 10-year Treasury yield captures the opportunity cost of holding gold, which does not generate interest. Rising yields increase the attractiveness of fixed-income assets relative to gold, while falling yields tend to support gold prices.

CPI (CPIAUCSL, monthly): FRED — fred.stlouisfed.org/series/CPIAUCSL
Why used: CPI measures inflation and purchasing-power erosion. Gold is widely viewed as an inflation hedge, making CPI a key variable for explaining long-run gold price behavior.

USD Index (DTWEXBGS, trade-weighted): FRED — fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DTWEXBGS
Why used: Gold is priced globally in U.S. dollars. A stronger USD generally puts downward pressure on gold prices, while a weaker USD supports demand. The trade-weighted index captures broad dollar strength rather than a narrow currency basket.

Geopolitical Risk (GPR): PolicyUncertainty — policyuncertainty.com/gpr.html (dataset: GPR_Data.csv )
Why used: GPR captures geopolitical shocks such as wars, conflicts, and political instability. These events often trigger flight-to-safety behavior, increasing demand for gold as a defensive asset.

VIX (risk sentiment proxy): FRED — fred.stlouisfed.org/series/VIXCLS (converted to monthly metric in our cleaning step)
Why used: The VIX measures market uncertainty and volatility. Elevated risk sentiment is commonly associated with increased safe-haven demand for gold.

Central Bank Gold Reserves (monthly statistics): World Gold Council — gold.org/goldhub/data/monthly-central-bank-statistics
Why used: Central bank purchases and sales represent structural, long-term demand for gold. Changes in reserve accumulation can shift gold’s equilibrium price and reflect changes in the global monetary regime.

Data Cleaning & Month-End Alignment

  • Month-end consistency: All variables are aligned to the same month-end timeline to avoid timing mismatch between prices, yields, indices, and macro indicators.
  • Cleaning: Missing values, inconsistent date stamps, and frequency mismatches are corrected so each regression observation uses fully comparable inputs.
  • Why month-end (not averages for level variables): Gold prices are spot levels determined by current conditions; using month-end values avoids mixing outdated information and reduces measurement error.

4. Methodology & Model Iteration

The final model is a multiple linear regression where the dependent variable is the gold price level (USD/oz), explained by macro and risk drivers. We tested an initial “real rate” approach, then improved interpretability and performance by separating CPI and the nominal 10Y yield.

Initial attempt (derived Real Rate): Real Rate was computed from nominal yield and inflation proxy, then used as a driver. This worked mechanically but produced weaker fit/interpretability in practice.

Final decision: Replace Real Rate with CPI + US10Y Nominal separately. This reduces noise from a constructed variable and clarifies the model’s economic channels.

Final specification (drivers): US10Y Nominal, CPI, USD Index, GPR, VIX, and Central Bank Gold Reserves.

Training vs testing discipline: Coefficients are trained on the in-sample period and then applied forward in the out-of-sample period without retraining to preserve a valid evaluation framework.

Interpretation note (important)

  • Out-of-sample errors post-2020 can reflect structural breaks (COVID stimulus, inflation regime shift, geopolitics, central bank demand), not “bad Excel.”

5. Backtesting & Validation

Validation is performed by comparing predicted gold price levels against actual month-end gold prices. We report error metrics and interpret when/why the model diverges (especially during regime changes).

In-sample: Used for estimation and calibration (Jan 2006 – Dec 2019).

Out-of-sample: Used for true performance evaluation (Jan 2020 – Jun 2025).

Error lens: Divergence can increase during shocks and structural breaks; this is economically interpretable.

6. Supply Chain Relevance

Gold forecasting supports supply chain decision-making for organizations exposed to commodity risk, currency regimes, and geopolitical disruptions—especially where procurement timing and hedging policies affect total cost.

Procurement planning: Better timing of purchases for gold-linked inputs and materials.

Hedging strategy: Informing hedge triggers and the timing of financial protection.

Risk management: Monitoring macro + geopolitical conditions that affect commodity costs.

Policy & reserves lens: Understanding how reserve accumulation and risk events influence demand.

7. Limitations & Enhancements

The model is intentionally transparent and reproducible, but it also carries constraints typical of monthly, linear macro-factor forecasting—especially when structural breaks occur.

Linearity: Relationships can change under extreme market stress or regime shifts.

Monthly frequency: Strong for macro structure, but may miss fast-moving within-month events.

Structural breaks: Post-2020 regimes can cause systematic level underprediction.

Enhancements: Scenario design, robustness checks, and alternative specifications can improve stability.
